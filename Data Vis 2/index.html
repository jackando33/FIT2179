International Tourism in Australia

Exploring Australia's tourism trends, arrivals and departures from 2000–2025

Author: Jack Anderson

Australian Tourism (2000-2025)

This dashboard visualises tourism to and from Australia between 2000 and 2025, highlighting international arrivals, outbound travel and Australia’s changing connections with countries around the world. Through interactive maps and charts, visitors can explore how tourism patterns have shifted over time and compare movement across countries, regions and seasons.

Together, these visualisations reveal Australia’s strongest tourism relationships and broader global travel trends over the past twenty-five years. From long-term growth and seasonal patterns to country-level comparisons and travel flows, the dashboard provides a clear overview of Australia’s place within the global tourism network.

1. Australia Tourism at a Glance

Australia’s international tourism grew steadily between 2000 and 2025, with both arrivals and departures showing a clear positive trend. This consistent growth highlights Australia’s increasing connection with global tourism, as more visitors are arriving in Australia and more Australians are

travelling overseas. This trend was interrupted in 2020 during the COVID-19 pandemic, when international travel dropped sharply. Following the reopening of borders, tourism rebounded strongly before continuing on a similar long-term growth trend through to 2025.

2. Where Tourists Come From

This choropleth map highlights Australia’s international tourism connections by showing the total number of arrivals and departures per 100,000 residents for each country between 2000 and 2025. Grouping countries into colour ranges makes it easier to compare tourism intensity relative to population size. Australia remains the darkest throughout the period, reflecting consistently high international travel. A clear shift occurs in 2020 and 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic, when most countries become lighter as global travel declined, before strengthening again as borders reopened. Following this recovery, Canada becomes one of Australia’s strongest tourism connections in the post-pandemic years.

This flow map visualises Australia’s top 50 international tourism connections between 2000 and 2025, with users able to switch between arrivals and departures and scroll through each year. Line thickness represents traveller volume, while colour coding highlights tourism connections across each continent. New Zealand, the United Kingdom, the United States and Brazil remain some of Australia’s strongest tourism links throughout the period. Across Asia, travel is initially strongest with Japan before shifting toward China in later years. A noticeable decline appears in 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic before tourism flows strengthen again.

This animated bar chart highlights the top 10 international tourism partners connected with Australia between 2000 and 2025, with users able to switch between arrivals and departures and scroll through each year. The changing rankings show how Australia’s strongest tourism relationships have shifted over time while also revealing several consistent travel partners. New Zealand remains Australia’s leading tourism connection throughout the full period, while the United Kingdom stays near the top despite gradually moving from second to third. Across Asia, Japan begins as one of Australia’s strongest tourism markets before being overtaken by China, while India also rises into the top rankings in later years.

3. How Tourism Changes Over Time

This streamgraph shows international tourism to and from Australia between 2000 and 2025, grouped by continent and excluding Australian citizens to better highlight overseas visitor patterns. Colour coding by continent reveals that most tourism originates from Asia, followed by Europe and then Oceania, showing the dominant global sources of travel into Australia over time. The overall pattern peaks in 2019, followed by a sharp narrowing in 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic, before expanding again as international travel recovers toward 2025.

This bubble chart compares international arrivals and departures between Australia and individual countries from 2000 to 2025, with each bubble representing a country. Both axes use a logarithmic scale, while bubble size is proportional to population and colours identify each continent. In the early years, countries are more spread out, with some showing higher arrivals and others higher departures. Over time, the bubbles move upward and become more closely aligned, indicating stronger and more balanced tourism connections. A small disruption appears in 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic, before returning to an almost linear pattern by 2025. Users can also filter the chart by continent or country population to explore travel patterns in more detail.

4. Tourism Across Australia

This visualisation explores tourism across Australia through a proportional symbol map and linked area chart between 2000 and 2025. The map highlights each state and territory using scaled circles, with four size groups representing the magnitude of arrivals or departures. As users move through each year and switch between arrivals and departures, the circles grow or shrink to reflect changing tourism activity across Australia. This provides a clear comparison of how tourism is distributed between states and territories over time.


The area chart beneath the map complements this by showing the contribution each state and territory makes to Australia’s overall tourism total. Because both charts update together using the shared year slider, users can compare changes in regional tourism patterns while also seeing how each state contributes to national tourism trends. Together, the two visualisations highlight the changing distribution of tourism across Australia over the past twenty-five years.


This seasonal heatmap highlights monthly tourism patterns to and from Australia between 2000 and 2025, with users able to switch between arrivals and departures. Darker colours represent higher tourism activity, revealing clear seasonal trends across the year. For arrivals, tourism remains strongest in December, January, July and October, while departures are highest in December, January, April and September. A noticeable bright band appears across 2020 and 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic, reflecting the sharp decline in international travel. Tourism activity strengthens again after border reopening, returning to darker seasonal patterns by 2025.

5. Key Tourism Insights

This treemap highlights the top 70 countries connected with Australia in 2025, based on total tourism activity combining arrivals and departures. Each rectangle is sized by tourism volume and colour coded by continent, with users also able to filter the view by continent. Australia remains the largest tourism market overall, while New Zealand stands out as Australia’s strongest international tourism connection. Across Asia, countries such as China, India and Japan contribute a large share of tourism activity, while the United Kingdom and United States remain major long-term travel partners. Together, the treemap provides a clear snapshot of Australia’s strongest tourism relationships in 2025 and how they are distributed across the world.

This diverging bar chart compares Australia’s top 10 tourism partners in 2025, showing departures to the left and arrivals to the right for each country. Users can scroll through each year to explore how tourism volumes change over time, while the mirrored layout makes it easy to compare inbound and outbound travel for each country. New Zealand and

China remain Australia’s strongest tourism connections, with consistently high tourism volumes throughout the period. Several countries also show clear differences between arrivals and departures, highlighting changes in travel demand and Australia’s strongest international tourism relationships between 2000 and 2025.

Conclusion

Australia’s tourism patterns between 2000 and 2025 highlight the country’s strong and evolving connection with the rest of the world. International tourism followed a steady long-term upward trend, interrupted only by the sharp decline during the COVID-19 pandemic before recovering strongly by 2025. New Zealand remained Australia’s most consistent tourism partner throughout the period, while the United Kingdom and United States remained major long-term travel connections. Across Asia, tourism also

shifted over time, with Japan strongest in the early years before China and India became more prominent. Together, these visualisations show clear seasonal peaks in December and January, strong tourism concentration across Australia’s major states, and lasting international connections across Oceania, Asia and Europe. Overall, the dashboard highlights Australia’s position as a major destination and travel hub within the global tourism network over the past twenty-five years.
